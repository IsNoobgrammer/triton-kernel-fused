An optimizer, explained from zero

ManasGIVING MUON A MEMORY

No background needed. We start at "what is training," build up through Adam and Muon, look at what the Nexus method wanted, and arrive at Manas — a small memory that lets the optimizer look where it's going before it steps.

00
The terrain

Training is walking downhill

Start with nothing assumed. A neural network is just a big pile of numbers — call them the parameters. Millions of them. Training is the process of nudging those numbers until the model gets good at its job.

To nudge them the right way you need to measure how wrong the model currently is. That measurement is a single number called the loss — high when the model is bad, low when it's good. Training has exactly one goal: make the loss small.

Here's the useful picture. Imagine every possible setting of the parameters spread out on a floor, and at each spot the height of the ground is the loss for that setting. That surface is the loss landscape: deep valleys where the model does well, high ridges where it fails. Training is a walk on this landscape, always trying to get downhill to a low valley.

Two facts make the walk hard, and every method below is a response to them. First, you're blindfolded — you can only feel the slope right under your feet, never see the whole map. That local slope is the gradient: the direction of steepest uphill, so you step the opposite way. Second, you don't even feel the true slope. You feel it on a small random sample of data called a batch, so the ground trembles a little differently every step. Your compass is noisy.

01
Gradient descent · Adam

The oldest move, and the fix everyone uses

The simplest strategy is gradient descent: feel the slope, take a step straight downhill, repeat. In a clean round bowl this works beautifully. Real loss valleys are not round bowls — they're long, narrow ravines: steep across, almost flat along the length. Step by the raw slope in a ravine and you mostly feel the steep side walls, so you bounce wall-to-wall while barely inching toward the far end.

Adam is the fix nearly everyone uses. It gives every single parameter its own step size. For each parameter it keeps a running record of how big that parameter's slope has recently been, and divides the step by it. A direction that has been steep gets damped; a direction that has been gentle gets amplified. The net effect: the ricochet across the ravine is tamed and progress along it speeds up.

02
Muon · orthogonalization

Update the whole matrix, not each number alone

Adam treats every number as a lonely knob with no relationship to its neighbors. But the weights of a layer aren't a loose pile — they form a matrix, a single geometric object that stretches some input directions a lot and others barely at all. Its gradient inherits that lopsidedness: a few directions carry almost all the push, the rest are faint. Step on it directly and you dump nearly your entire update into a handful of directions and starve the others.

Muon takes the whole matrix in hand at once. Before stepping, it orthogonalizes the update — a short numerical routine that levels every one of the matrix's characteristic strengths (its "singular values") to one. Geometrically: the raw update sends a circle to a lopsided ellipse; Muon rounds that ellipse back into a circle. Every direction gets an equal, confident push. Adam rescales numbers; Muon rebalances geometry — and for the same compute it reaches better models.

Our foundation: Aurora-Muon, so no neuron goes dark

Plain orthogonalization has a quiet flaw. On tall matrices the per-row strengths drift apart, and some rows — some neurons — stop receiving a real update and effectively die. Aurora-Muon, the version we build on, prescales the rows before orthogonalizing and re-orthogonalizes, so every row keeps a genuine signal and the whole spectrum stays even. That's the healthy base Manas is grafted onto: a Muon where every neuron stays alive.

03
The real prize

Same loss, but a better model

Here's the subtle thing that started this whole project. Two models can reach the exact same training loss and still behave very differently on data they've never seen. What separates them is often where they landed. A sharp, narrow pit fits the training data and little else. A broad basin — a place where many different batches of data all agree this is a good spot — tends to generalize far better.

Now recall the compass is noisy: each batch draws a slightly different landscape with its own lowest point. Chase whichever batch is in front of you and you settle into a cramped compromise pit. Steer instead toward the region where the batches agree — the common minimum — and you land in the broad, robust basin. That is the prize: identical training loss, but a model that holds up in the real world.

04
Nexus · the prior method

How Nexus reaches the common minimum

Nexus is the published method that first chased the common minimum during training, built on Adam. Its idea is clever, and understanding it exactly is what makes Manas's shortcut obvious. Nexus runs a small rehearsal before each real step.

  1. Clone the model

    Make a full copy of every parameter — a scratch model to experiment on, so the real one is never disturbed.

  2. Walk the clone across several batches

    Take a handful of batches one at a time. For each, compute its slope on the clone and take a small, length-normalized step. The clone drifts toward wherever these batches jointly pull — toward their common ground.

  3. Measure how far the clone travelled

    Subtract the clone's new position from where it started. That displacement vector points toward the common minimum. Nexus calls it the meta-gradient — a gradient assembled not from one batch but from the agreement of several.

  4. Feed it to Adam, then resync

    Hand that meta-gradient to Adam as if it were an ordinary slope, let Adam take the real step, and copy the real model back onto the clone to start over.

It works — on Adam. The catch is that the whole signal lives in the lengths of that displacement vector: directions the batches agreed on come out long, contested ones come out short. Adam respects those lengths. Muon does not — its orthogonalization deliberately flattens all lengths to equal, erasing exactly the information Nexus worked to build. Bolt Nexus onto Muon and the two fight; the reported result is that it simply doesn't help. That collision is the problem Manas had to route around.

05
The key idea

Looking ahead before you step

Before Manas itself, one idea needs its own moment, because everything hinges on it: looking ahead. It is the difference between reading a map where you stand and reading it a step in the direction you're already heading.

Picture a curving valley. The slope right where you stand points at the near wall — follow it and you climb the bank of the bend. But the slope a short step ahead, around the curve, points down the length of the valley. If you first hop to that look-ahead spot, read the slope there, and use that reading, you anticipate the turn instead of getting surprised by it. You commit to the direction that will still be right once you've moved.

The obvious question: look ahead in which direction? A single noisy batch is a bad guide. Manas's answer is what makes it Manas.

06
Manas

The consensus probe, step by step

Manas looks ahead along the direction recent batches have agreed on, and reads the slope there. It keeps a short, fading memory of where the last several batches pulled — each counted as a single equal vote, so their random noise cancels and their shared direction survives. Call that memory d. Then it does the look-ahead: step a little along d, read the slope at that spot, and hand that reading to Aurora-Muon to orthogonalize and step on.

  1. Remember where batches have been pulling

    Keep a running, fading sum of recent gradient directions — each normalized to one equal vote. Agreement builds up; noise averages away. This memory is d, and it points toward the common ground.

  2. Look ahead along that memory

    Don't read the slope where you stand. Step a small amount — set by γ — along d to a probe spot, out toward where the batches agree.

  3. Read the slope there — for free

    Take this step's normal batch and compute its gradient at the probe spot instead of at the current point. Crucially this is the same gradient computation training does anyway — no clone, no extra passes. You just measured it one step further along.

  4. Let Aurora-Muon step on it

    Hand that look-ahead gradient to Muon. Orthogonalization only rotates a direction — it can't erase the consensus, because the consensus is carried in the direction, not the lengths. This is exactly why Manas rides Muon where Nexus couldn't.

The honest part: what we expected vs what we measured

We began sure that Manas was rebuilding the same agreement signal Nexus uses. We built the measurement to confirm it — and it said no. That signal is injected most cleanly with no memory at all, precisely the setting that does nothing useful; meanwhile the weights move so far between steps that the tidy theory never even applies. What actually drives the win, found by switching each piece off one at a time: a long memory, a downhill look-ahead, and a momentum-free direction. The truthful name isn't "agreement force" — it's consensus-direction look-ahead. The frozen test, never our intuition, got the final word.

07
Anatomy

Four knobs — two of them aren't really knobs

dt = −γ · Σk ρk · gt−k / ‖gt−k‖a fading (ρ) sum of unit-length recent gradients (each an equal vote), scaled by γ — then read the slope at θ + dt
d
consensus memory

The fading, equal-vote average of recent gradient directions — this is the look-ahead direction itself. Because it's a heavily smoothed direction it lives in a tiny subspace, so we store it compressed (rank 8) for about 1% of a model copy and lose nothing measurable.

γ
gamma · how far to look

How big a step along d before reading the slope — the reach of the look-ahead. It depends on the landscape's local scale (learning rate, weight sizes, curvature), so it's the one knob you genuinely tune per model. The response is smooth with a wide safe range.

ρ
rho · how long to remember

How fast old votes fade. Not a free number: it's memory measured in samples. Window × batch size stays roughly constant, so tiny noisy batches want a long memory and huge batches want a short one. Derive it from your batch size — don't guess.

u
update memory · optional

A second fading memory — of the steps the optimizer has actually taken — added to the look-ahead so it also anticipates realized travel. Off by default, free to carry at rank 8. On our small test it rode along without helping or hurting; heterogeneous large-scale data is where it should earn its place.

How we got here & what it did

The path, and the numbers

START

Aurora-Muon — every neuron alive

Row-prescaled, re-orthogonalized Muon so no row loses its signal. A healthy, even base to build on.

GRAFT

The look-ahead probe, ported off Adam

Carry the common-minimum signal in the gradient's direction, where orthogonalization can only rotate it — never erase it. And with no clone and no extra passes.

TEST

Switch each piece off, one at a time

No memory: nothing. Uphill look-ahead: nothing. Looking along Muon's own momentum: nothing. Only the long-memory, downhill, momentum-free direction carried the win — and our first tidy theory didn't survive the measurement.

SCALE

The memory is a law; the reach is a dial

ρ turned out to be memory-in-samples, confirmed across a 16× batch-size sweep. γ is the single dial to set per model. Everything else has a validated default.

SHRINK

The signal lives in a tiny subspace

Rank-8 storage kept the full effect at about 1% of a model copy — strong evidence the mechanism is a smooth, low-dimensional direction, and the rest was noise worth discarding.

Where this stands

This is a controlled small-scale result, honestly gated — not yet a large language-model claim. The two things that settle it at scale are already set up: derive ρ from batch size using the memory law, tune only γ, and A/B the optional u memory where the data is varied enough for it to matter. That run is the next step.
